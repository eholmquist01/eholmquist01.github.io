MCM Competition

Sustainable Tourism Modeling in Alaska

Competed in a team-based mathematical modeling competition where we developed a differential equation model to analyze the environmental and economic impact of cruise tourism in Juneau, Alaska. Our goal was to design a sustainable tourism framework that balanced revenue generation with a 60% reduction in carbon dioxide emissions, while respecting the city’s 16,000 visitor daily cap.

We constructed a system of differential equations incorporating logistic growth and linear functions to model tourism growth, revenue optimization, environmental degradation, and policy constraints. By analyzing variables such as tourist spending, carbon emissions per visitor, and tourism growth and loss rates, we identified strategies that would allow Juneau to increase revenue while reducing its environmental footprint.

Our model demonstrated how strategic pricing adjustments and reinvestment into eco-friendly infrastructure could simultaneously preserve natural resources and sustain economic growth. We also explored how this framework could be adapted to other tourism-dependent destinations with different capacity and environmental constraints.

This project strengthened my experience in mathematical modeling, sustainability analysis, and translating complex quantitative results into actionable policy recommendations.

Saint Mary's Sustainable Farm Analysis

Analyzing the Social Impact of the Local Sustainable Farm

This project examined what drives community members to attend the Saint Mary’s sustainable farm market. Working in a team, we collaboratively designed the survey and cleaned the raw data to ensure accurate and meaningful results. Individually, I analyzed the survey responses to explore how produce quality, variety, and quantity influenced attendance, considering different roles within the campus community (students, faculty, staff, and others) and attendance frequency.

My analysis revealed that most market attendees are staff or on-campus students, with quality of produce generally valued more than variety or quantity. Attendance frequency did not strongly affect preferences, and nearly all respondents agreed that the market is an important resource. While some statistical tests were limited by survey design constraints, these insights highlighted key factors that contribute to community engagement and the farm’s social impact.

I also proposed survey redesign improvements to allow more independent ranking of produce attributes, which would enable deeper statistical analysis in future iterations. This project demonstrated how collaborative efforts in data collection and cleaning, combined with individual analysis, can provide actionable insights to support local sustainability initiatives.
